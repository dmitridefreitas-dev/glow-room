# The problem we're actually solving

> North star. Read this before building anything. Every version in `concepts/`
> is judged against this, not against "is it cool" or "does it have features."

## Where this comes from
The Glow Room exists because the founder lived it: a long stretch with crippling
depression and **no structure** — couldn't get out of bed, make the bed, make
breakfast, take out the trash. Psychologists and psychiatrists didn't move the
needle. What finally did was **building a structure for the day, from scratch,
one small piece at a time** — earned over years of trial and error.

The mission: hand that structure to the next person so it doesn't take them years.

## Who it's for
- Gen Z, often college students.
- Depression / low motivation / no routine.
- Sleeping through the day, skipping class, feeling worse for it.
- Can't reliably do "basic" things: get up, eat, shower, tidy, show up.
- They are paying for **structure** — a concrete path out.

## The job to be done
> "Give me a structure for my day so I can function, and walk me through it one
> tiny step at a time — without overwhelming me or making me feel like a failure."

## The non-negotiables (design rules for this user)
1. **One thing at a time.** Never a list. A depressed brain reads a list as proof
   of everything it can't do. Show the single next small step. Nothing else.
2. **Micro-steps.** "Make breakfast" is too big. "Sit up → feet on the floor →
   open the curtains → glass of water → make toast." Granularity is the whole
   trick — and it's exactly what the founder's templates encode.
3. **Structure, provided.** The user shouldn't have to design their day — that's
   the thing they can't do. The app brings the scaffold. A blank planner fails them.
4. **Zero shame, infinite forgiveness.** No punishing streaks, no "you broke it,"
   no rank to lose. Missing is normal: "welcome back — next small thing."
5. **Quiet, real wins.** Each done is genuine evidence: *you are capable.* End the
   day on "you did 5 things — that's you climbing," never "you failed 3."
6. **Lowest possible friction.** Open → see the one step → Done. No setup mazes,
   no choices that stall, no navigation between the user and the next action.

## Why simple wins here (the Apple / Tesla point)
For this user, complexity isn't a feature — it's a barrier. The most *capable*
product loses to the most *frictionless* one. Our edge is ruthless simplicity +
lived-experience structure, not feature count.

## The clinical backbone (so we build on something real)
In plain terms this is **Behavioral Activation (BA)** — a first-line,
evidence-based approach to depression: break the withdrawal/avoidance spiral with
small, scheduled, achievable actions that rebuild momentum and self-efficacy. BA's
core truth fits perfectly: **action first, motivation follows — you don't wait to
feel like it.** We are NOT therapy and we make no medical claims; we're a
structure-and-momentum companion built on the same principle.

## What we are NOT building (for this mission)
- A feature-rich self-improvement game you have to "play."
- Anything that shames, ranks, or pressures.
- A blank planner that offloads the hard part (designing structure) onto the user.
- "Glow up / aesthetic" as the core promise. It's a nice surface, not the job.
